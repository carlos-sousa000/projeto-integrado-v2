Direct Multiplication - Edmund Barbour

Edmund Barbour created a patent for a calculator that divided the multiplier into parts (tens, units, hundreds, etc.) and then multiplied each one individually.

Overview In 1872, Edmund Barbour patented a calculator that performed direct multiplications through a system of numbered cylinders. Each cylinder represented a decimal place (units, tens, hundreds, etc.), and the machine multiplied each part separately, automatically adding the results.

Historical Context

In 1872, Edmund Barbour had an idea for a multiplication calculator, but lacking the money or engineering skill to build it himself, he created a patent instead.

How did it work?

It had cylinders for each place value (units, tens, hundreds, etc.) and to multiply it would calculate one digit of the multiplier at a time and then add. For example: 10 x 22, the machine would calculate 10 x 2 = 20 and 10 x 2 = 20 (shift to tens place) becomes 200, and 200 + 20 = 220
