Tabulating Machine - Herman Hollerith

Herman Hollerith developed a punched card system and an electromechanical tabulating machine, speeding up the 1890 US census and ushering in the era of automatic data processing, the foundation of the future information industry.

Historical Context

At the end of the 19th century, population and administrative growth made manual data tabulation unfeasible. Hollerith was inspired by punched railway tickets to create a mechanical-electrical solution that recorded and counted information quickly and accurately.

Contributions to History

Hollerith created punched cards with positions for attributes (age, sex, state, etc.) and an electromechanical tabulator that read them via electrical contacts, allowing millions of records to be processed much faster than traditional manual counting.

Importance of the Inventor

After the success with the census, Hollerith founded the Tabulating Machine Company (in the 19th century). This company became part of what we know today as IBM, and punched card technology remained central to data processing for decades. The idea of encoding information on a physical medium and automating its reading directly influenced the development of computers and information systems.
