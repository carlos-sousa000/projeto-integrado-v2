Timeline — from Babbage to Hollerith

Explore the historical sequence of computing pioneers.

Difference Engine — Charles Babbage

Designed to automatically calculate and print mathematical tables. It was fully mechanical, with gears and wheels — a fundamental step towards the modern computer.

Semyon Korsakov — Card Storage

Used punched cards and registers to organize information, anticipating mechanical data indexing systems.

Calculator with keyboard — Torchi

Early examples of machines with keyboard input, which facilitated the direct insertion of numbers.

Electromechanical Relay — Joseph Henry

Electromechanical components that allowed signal control and predated logic circuits.

Analytical Engine — Charles Babbage

Concept of a general-purpose computer: arithmetic unit, memory, control, and use of cards for instructions — conceived, but not fully built at the time.

Ada Lovelace — First published algorithm

Notes on the Analytical Engine that included an algorithm to calculate Bernoulli numbers. Ada Lovelace is recognized as the first programmer.

The Relational Machine — Alfred Smee

Proposed machines to mechanically represent and compare ideas — influential ideas, although rarely built.

Boolean Algebra and symbolic logic

George Boole formalized logic and allowed logical operations to be treated algebraically — an essential basis for digital circuits.

Telettrofono — Antonio Meucci

Distance communication device that used wires and electricity, considered a forerunner to the modern telephone.

Single Column Calculator — Caroline Winter

Simple device created to facilitate arithmetic calculations in a single column, contributing to the evolution of mechanical calculators.

The Typewriter

Mechanical device that allowed writing texts more quickly and efficiently, revolutionizing written communication.

The Logical Piano — William Stanley Jevons

Symbolic logic system proposed by William Stanley Jevons, which sought to represent logical reasoning in a clearer and more structured way.

Direct Multiplication — Edmund Barbour

Method proposed by Edmund Barbour to simplify multiplications, with a more direct approach.

Frege's Logic — Gottlob Frege

Frege published the book Begriffsschrift, which introduced a symbolic notation for logic. His work profoundly influenced modern logic, making mathematics and philosophy more precise.

NAND and NOR — Charles Peirce

Charles Peirce demonstrated how to emulate various logical operators using only two (NAND and NOR), ideas that played an important role in the development of binary logical systems.

Brazilian Calculator — Azevedo Coutinho

Unfinished project for a calculator, conceptualized by a Brazilian during the Industrial Revolution.

Tabulating Machine — Herman Hollerith

Used punched cards and electromechanical readers to speed up the processing of the US census — a milestone in data automation and the origin of the Tabulating Machine Company (future IBM).
