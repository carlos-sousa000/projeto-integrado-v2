Timeline — from Babbage to Hollerith

Explore the historical sequence of computing pioneers.

Differential Engine — Charles Babbage

Designed to calculate and print mathematical tables automatically. It was fully mechanical, using gears and wheels — a fundamental step toward the modern computer.

Semyon Korsakov — Card-based storage

Used punched cards and registrars to organize information, anticipating mechanical data indexing systems.

Keyboard Calculator — Torchi

Early examples of machines with keyboard input, which made direct numerical entry easier.

Electromechanical Relay — Joseph Henry

Electromechanical components that enabled signal control and foreshadowed logic circuits.

Analytical Engine — Charles Babbage

Concept of a general-purpose computer: arithmetic unit, memory, control, and the use of cards for instructions — designed but not fully built at the time.

Ada Lovelace — First published algorithm

Notes on the Analytical Engine included an algorithm to compute Bernoulli numbers. Ada Lovelace is recognized as the first programmer.

The Relational Machine — Alfred Smee

Proposed machines to mechanically represent and compare ideas — influential concepts, though rarely built.

Boolean Algebra and symbolic logic

George Boole formalized logic and allowed logical operations to be treated algebraically — an essential basis for digital circuits.

Telettrophone — Antonio Meucci

A distance communication device using wires and electricity, considered a precursor to the modern telephone.

Single-Column Calculator — Caroline Winter

A simple device created to ease single-column arithmetic calculations, contributing to the evolution of mechanical calculators.

The Typewriter

A mechanical device that allowed faster and more efficient text writing, revolutionizing written communication.

The Logical Plan — William Stanley Jevons

A symbolic logic system proposed by William Stanley Jevons, aiming to represent reasoning more clearly and structurally.

Direct Multiplication — Edmund Barbour

A method proposed by Edmund Barbour to simplify multiplications using a more direct approach.

Frege's Logic — Gottlob Frege

Frege published Begriffsschrift, introducing a symbolic notation for logic. His work deeply influenced modern logic, making mathematics and philosophy more precise.

NE and NOR — Charles Peirce

Charles Peirce showed how to emulate various logical operators using only two (NE and NOR), ideas important to the development of binary logic systems.

Brazilian Calculator — Azevedo Coutinho

An unfinished calculator project conceived by a Brazilian inventor during the Industrial Revolution.

Tabulating Machine — Herman Hollerith

Used punched cards and electromechanical readers to speed up processing of the U.S. census — a milestone in data automation and the origin of the Tabulating Machine Company (later IBM).
