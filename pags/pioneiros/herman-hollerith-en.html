Herman Hollerith (1860–1929)

Herman Hollerith (1860–1929) was an American engineer and inventor. He worked at the U.S. Census Bureau and faced the challenge of processing large volumes of data manually. He graduated in engineering and was a professor at Columbia University. He sought practical solutions for administrative and statistical problems. He founded a company that would become IBM.

How He Influenced History

Hollerith revolutionized data processing by introducing large-scale automation. His system drastically reduced the time needed to tabulate the U.S. census. He inspired the use of technology in public administration and business. He was a pioneer in the information age even before the electronic computer. His business model shaped the 20th-century technology industry.

Achievements Throughout His Life

He invented the tabulating machine that used punched cards to record and process data. He developed automatic card punches, readers, and sorters. He created an integrated system for data collection, storage, and analysis. He patented his machine in 1889. He founded the Tabulating Machine Company, a forerunner of IBM.

How His Inventions/Achievements Helped History

His system was used in the 1890 U.S. census, reducing processing time from years to months. It made handling large databases feasible for government, industry, and commerce. Punched cards were used for decades in computers until the 1970s. He paved the way for business and government automation. He is considered one of the fathers of modern computing.
